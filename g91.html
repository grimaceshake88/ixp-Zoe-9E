What is Dynamic Electricity?

Dynamic electricity is the flow of electric charges through a conductor. These flows of electric charges are called current (I).



Ohms Law:

It explains the how relationship between the current that flows through most conductors is directionally proportional to the voltage (V) applied. It’s seen as a cause-and-effect relationship as the voltage is the cause and the current is the relationship.

If voltage drives current, resistance (R/ohms/Ω) impedes it. It opposes the flow of current in the electrical circuit. When the resistance doubles, the current is cut in half.

Combining all of these relationships gives us the following formula;

Formula:
Current = Voltage/Resistance
Resistance = Voltage/Current
Voltage = Current x Resistance

Types of Circuit:

1. Series Circuit
Series circuits have no branches and only have one path the current can follow.
If multiple light bulbs are connected in a series circuit, then it share the same voltage and get increasingly dimmer as more lightbulbs are added.
If any one of the light bulbs breaks, then all of the bulbs in the circuit will not function as the circuit opens and no current will flow.

When resistors are connected in a series circuit, then it adds up.


Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + R4



2. Parallel Circuit
Parallel circuits, unlike series, have multiple branches and paths for the current to flow.
If lightbulbs are connected in parallel circuits, then they each have the same voltage and do not decrease if another is added.
If a bulb breaks, then the others will work as the current can flow through the other branches.

Movement of Charge:

When present in an electric field, a positive charge will naturally move in the direction of the electric field. This is because the electric field will exert a force on the charge which is the same direction as the electric field.








Meanwhile, if a negative charge is placed in a magnetic field,
it will move in the opposite direction of the electric field.
This is because the electric field also exerts a force onto the negative charge which causes it to go the opposite of the electric field.


Kirchoff's Laws:

1. First Law
The sum of currents entering the junction will always equal the sum of currents leaving the junction.








2. Second Law
The total electromotive force (e.m.f) in a closed circuit is equal to the sum of potential differences in the circuit.
